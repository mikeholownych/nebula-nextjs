Why Your Landing Page Isn't Converting (And How to Fix It)

Most landing pages have the same 5 problems. Here's how to find and fix yours.

Quick Answer: Most landing pages fail for one of five reasons: the headline describes the product instead of the buyer's result, the CTA is buried or weak, there is no social proof near the form, the page loads slowly, or it breaks on mobile. Fix the headline first — it affects every visitor and often doubles conversion rate alone.

You've launched. You're getting traffic. But signups are flat, or worse — zero. The frustrating part: you can't tell why by staring at the page. Every founder thinks their copy is fine, their CTA is obvious, and their offer is clear. But conversion data tells a different story.

After auditing 50+ indie founder landing pages, the same five problems come up over and over. Here they are — with the specific fix for each.

1. Your Headline Doesn't Say What You Do

The problem

Most SaaS headlines are aspirational instead of descriptive. "Transform your workflow" or "The future of productivity" tell visitors nothing. A visitor who doesn't immediately understand what you do will leave in under 5 seconds — and they won't know why they left.

The fix

Your headline should answer: who it's for, what it does, and what outcome it delivers. Use this formula: [Audience] can [action] without [pain].

  1. Write 10 versions of your headline in 10 minutes
  2. Read each one to someone unfamiliar with your product — they should be able to explain it back
  3. The clearest one wins, not the cleverest

2. Your CTA Is Weak or Buried

The problem

The most common CTA mistake isn't bad copy — it's placement. "Sign up" below the fold gets ignored. Vague CTAs like "Learn more" or "Get started" create friction because they don't tell the visitor what happens next. Low-contrast buttons disappear against the background.

The fix
  1. Put your primary CTA above the fold — visible without scrolling on every device
  2. Use action + outcome language: "Start my free audit" not "Get started"
  3. Button color must pass WCAG AA contrast — test at contrast-ratio.com
  4. One primary CTA per page. Remove everything competing with it.

3. You Have No Social Proof (Or the Wrong Kind)

The problem

Visitors arrive skeptical. Logos without names, generic testimonials ("Great product!"), or fake-looking avatars actively destroy trust. Missing social proof is the #1 objection you're not answering. If you have zero customers, you're still not off the hook — there are alternatives.

The fix
  1. Real testimonials with full name, photo, and company are 3x more effective than anonymous ones
  2. Specificity converts: "Saved me 4 hours a week" beats "Really useful"
  3. No customers yet? Use: user count ("47 founders using this"), beta feedback quotes, or a "Built in public" transparency section
  4. Place social proof directly next to your CTA, not at the bottom

4. Your Page Loads Too Slowly

The problem

53% of mobile users abandon a page that takes longer than 3 seconds to load. Every extra second of load time costs you roughly 7% of conversions. Most founders don't know their page speed — they test it on a fast connection and assume it's fine.

The fix
  1. Run your page through pagespeed.web.dev — target 90+ on mobile
  2. Compress all images with squoosh.app (free) and serve as WebP
  3. Remove any third-party scripts that aren't essential (chat widgets, analytics with 200ms+ blocking)
  4. Use a CDN. Cloudflare free tier adds 200-400ms improvement with zero effort.

5. It Breaks on Mobile

The problem

Over 60% of web traffic is mobile — but most indie founders build on desktop and never test on an actual phone. The most common failures: text too small to read without zooming, CTAs too small to tap, horizontal scroll that breaks layout, hero images that overflow their container.

The fix
  1. Test on a real phone, not just browser dev tools — they lie
  2. Minimum tap target size: 44×44px for any interactive element
  3. Minimum body font: 16px. Smaller requires pinch-to-zoom, which kills conversions.
  4. Use clamp() for responsive font sizes: font-size: clamp(1rem, 4vw, 1.2rem)
  5. Your hero image on mobile should either be hidden or explicitly sized — never allowed to overflow
